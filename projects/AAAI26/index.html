Towards Reliable Few-Shot Adaptation of Pathology Foundation Models via Conformal Prediction

Aditya Narendra1, Subhankar Panda1,2 & Chandresh Kumar Maurya1
1 Department of Computer Science and Engineering, IIT Indore, India 2 Odisha University of Technology and Research, India
AAAI 2026 (AIMedHealth Bridge Program)
Paper Code Poster Slides

TL;DR


  • We integrate Conformal Prediction (CP) methods with few-shot adaption of pathology foundation models to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, improving reliability and diagnostic efficiency.
  • We introduce four novel, clinically aligned evaluation metrics designed to assess the real-world effectiveness of CP approaches in few-shot diagnostic settings

    • Abstract

    Recent advances in foundation models have enabled their integration into high-stakes clinical settings, particularly in computational pathology, where domain-specialized FMs demonstrate strong generalization. However, real-world deployment is constrained by their poorly calibrated uncertainty awareness and degraded performance in low-data regimes requiring few-shot adaptation strategies, leading to unreliable and inefficient diagnostic workflows. Conformal Prediction (CP) is an uncertainty quantification framework that offers distribution‑free, finite‑sample coverage guarantees for ensuring safer deployment in such settings. In this work, we explore the integration of various CP methods with pathology foundation models using three few-shot adaption strategies for classification tasks across two datasets. To assess the clinical effectiveness of these approaches, we propose four novel metrics aimed at improving clinical reliability and alleviating diagnostic workload in few-shot settings. Our results demonstrate that Conformal Prediction methods enhance the reliability of pathology foundation models and offer actionable uncertainty estimates to enable safe and efficient deployment in few-shot pathological classification workflows, with the LAC method achieving the best overall performance.

    Methodology


    We integrate Conformal Prediction (CP) with few-shot adaptation of pathology foundation models (UNI, Phikon, and Hibou-B) to produce uncertainty-calibrated predictions in data-scarce clinical settings. Using three few-shot strategies: Baseline, Baseline++, and Prototypical Networks-support samples are encoded with frozen PFMs, while a separate calibration set is used to compute CP based nonconformity scores for LAC, APS, and RAPS methods. These scores generate prediction sets for query samples, providing actionable uncertainty estimates that account for diagnostic ambiguity and enable reliable few-shot classification without full model fine-tuning.

    Evaluation


    We evaluate the integrated CP and few-shot pathology foundation model framework across two datasets: HiCervix (cervical cells) and HMU-GC-HE-30K (gastric tissue) using 1, 5 and 10-shot settings. The models are evaluated with both standard CP metrics such as empirical coverage and average set size, and four novel clinically aligned metrics designed to reflect real-world utility: Mean Rank of True Label (MRTL), Rank to Correct (RTC), Rank 1 Containment Rate (R1CR), and Effective Singleton Rate (ESR). This evaluation strategy quantifies not only the reliability and calibration of prediction sets but also their impact on clinical workflow efficiency and diagnostic workload in few-shot pathological classification.

    Results

    Tables 1 and 2 on the HiCervix dataset show that LAC consistently produced the most well-calibrated and clinically efficient prediction sets across all few-shot methods and CP frameworks, followed by APS. LAC reduced the Mean Rank of True Label (MRTL) by up to 35% and Rank to Correct (RTC) by 50%, streamlining diagnostic workflows. ProtoNets achieved the most consistent label ranking, with up to 64% higher Rank 1 Containment Rate (R1CR) while maintaining strong ESR, with the best results obtained using LAC + ProtoNets + Phikon.

    Tables 3 and 4 on the HMU-GC-HE-30K dataset show that LAC consistently produced the most well-calibrated and clinically efficient prediction sets across all few-shot methods and CP frameworks, followed by APS. LAC reduced the Mean Rank of True Label (MRTL) by 15–30% and Rank to Correct (RTC) by 25–45% compared to APS, streamlining diagnostic workflows. Among few-shot strategies, Baseline achieved the most consistent label ranking and calibration, with up to 25% higher Rank 1 Containment Rate (R1CR) and up to 250% higher Effective Singleton Rate (ESR) than Baseline++ and ProtoNets. The best-performing configuration combined LAC + Baseline + Hibou-B, highlighting its potential to reduce clinical workload and improve patient outcomes.

    Overall across both the datasets, LAC consistently produced the most well-calibrated and clinically efficient prediction sets, followed by APS. Few-shot adaptation strategies showed varying strengths: ProtoNets excelled in true label ranking on HiCervix, while Baseline achieved the most robust calibration and highest singleton predictions on HMU-GC-HE-30K. Overall, combining LAC with the optimal few-shot method and foundation model for each dataset (ProtoNets + Phikon for HiCervix, Baseline + Hibou-B for HMU-GC-HE-30K) delivered the most reliable and clinically actionable predictions.
